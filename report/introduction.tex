It has become apparent within the recent year alone, that the development of large language models (LLMs) has improved the capabilities of artificial intelligence (AI), and whilst the performance of LLMs seems to be improving at a somewhat stagnating rate, the capabilities and uses of AI are continuously expanded upon. 

In August 2025, Anthropic's Claude model was used in one of, if not the first case of "vibe hacking" outlined in \fullcite{ThreatReport2025}. The hacker used the model to look for publicly available information about security vulnerabilities in software used by various organizations, find companies that utilized the software, and then exploited the bug to gain access to their internal infrastructure. The hack was unique in the sense that the hack was made with minimal human involvement, and the hacker was able to use the model to generate the necessary malware to extort the companies for a ransom.

If AI continues to develop at the current pace, it is likely that AI will be able to autonomously develop malware at the press of a button, even more sophisticated than previously seen, and at a much faster rate. This raises concerns about what the future of cybersecurity will look like, but one thing experts seem to agree on is that we need to utilize AI in this field as well, to be able to counteract malicious AI.

\subsection{The Problem}
\label{ResearchQuestion}

This raises the question of how should we counteract this malicious AI, in what ways can we take steps to mitigate the risks of it, and how can we train a model to be able to detect and counteract these threats?

The aim of this report is to investigate potential risks involved with the general development of AI, the nature of hacking with and without AI, and how we could find data to train a model so that it might be able to detect and counteract bad actors that might utilize AI for malicious purposes. The report will also explore the potential problems that may arise from training a model to detect and try to counteract another model, and how we should approach these problems, if they arise.

\subsubsection{Research Question}

The research question is then as follows:

\begin{center}
    \textit{How can an AI model be trained to detect and counteract bad actors that utilize AI for malicious purposes, and what are the potential problems that may arise from training such a model?}
\end{center}

The research question is focused on the training aspect of AI, as it is the core problem needs to be solved in this case. Data is the most important aspect of training an AI model, and in our case, it us not clear what that data would look like or how we would obtain it to begin with, as such this becomes the main focus of our research to uncover. 

For this, we will need to explore three main topics. The first being hacking in general, the second being AI and how it is being trained currently, and the third being the intersection of the two, which is "vibe hacking".

With this outline, we can ask ourselves the following sub-questions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item What are the current methods of training AI models?
    \item What are the potential challenges in training an AI model on data indirectly or directly related to another model?
    \item What problems may arise from training an AI to detect and counteract malicious AI, and how can we mitigate these problems?
\end{itemize}
